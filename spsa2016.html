Flows: Fluidity

  • The extent to which goods can flow through the network
    • How far can a good travel?
    • Can a partner's partner directly gain from the good?
Signal Loss: Otherwise fluid goods such as information may lose fidelity as they are passed along the network.

Examples:

  • High Fluidity
    • Arms Trade
    • Intelligence
    • Training
  • High Viscosity
    • Recognition
    • Infrastructure
    • Joint Operations

Flows: Value

How valuable is the good to the members of the network?


  • Substitutability
  • Prevalence
  • Profitability/Spoils

  • Level of Investment
    • Time
    • Funds
    Tying it Together: Viscosity can reduce the availability of goods, giving them a higher value.
    Tying it Together: Low value goods may be attained independently by groups, regardless of fluidity.

    Flows: Transaction Costs

    • Cost of Doing Business
      • Time
      • Travel
      • Transactions
      • Investment
    Tying it Together:More fluid goods generally have lower transaction costs.
    • Opportunity Cost
      • What else could be done with the time and resources going into this tie?
    • Risk
      • Does the interaction require secrecy?
      • What are the consequences of law enforcement discovering the interaction?

    Actors: Assets & Security

    • Assets
      • Natural Resources
      • Skills
      • Support Base
      • Rents
    Tying it Together:Well established groups, and groups with territory, have lower transaction costs.

    Security


    • Territory
      • Control over territory or access to a safe haven reduces risks inherent to illicit ties
    • Entrenchment
      • Well entrenched groups with strong support bases face fewer risks and can operate more openly
      • Is the group established?
      • Are they strong enough to survive?

    Environment: Polarity & Proximity

    Polarity

    • Anarchy
      • Failed State
      • Ungoverned Territory
      • Compete with other groups
    • Hegemony
      • Strong State
      • Leviathan
      • Common Enemy
      • Prisoner's Dilemma
    • Transitional

    Proximity

    • Same territory
    • Adjacent
    • Long-distance

    Tying it Together: Proximate groups and groups in anarchy have lower transaction costs.

    Constraints: Saturation & Unraveling

    Saturation

    • High Transaction Costs
      • Limited Resources means limited alliances
      • Varies with assets
    • Attentional deficits
      • When too many ties are formed, they all suffer

    Unraveling

    • When law enforcement uses information gained from infiltrating or dismantling one group against its partners.
    • Risky Transactions
    • Tension between robustness and security
    Preferential Attachment: Limited alliances means more selectivity.

    Constraints: Commitment Problems

    Tit-for-Tat

    • Low fluidity, high value
      • Higher leverage
      • Cannot circumvent
      • Punishment possible
    • Overlapping partnerships
      • Collective Punishment
      • High assets, or high leverage (low fluidity high value)
    High Transitivity: Decentralized network with overlapping partnerships.

    Central Arbiter

    • High fluidity, low value
      • Low leverage
      • Get access to flows from other neighbors
    • Preferential attachment around trusted central arbiter
      • Security autonomy tradeoff
      • High punishment, high effort networks
    Low Transitivity: Centralized hub-and-spoke network with well endowed centers.

    Game: Simulating Alliance Aggregation

    Order of Operations

    1. Random Tie Formation:Choose whether to accept
    2. Transaction Costs:Pay for all formed ties
    3. Play Game:Ultimatum Game or Prisoner's Dilemma
    4. Receive Payoff:From partners with mutual cooperation, and their neighborhoods, depending on fluidity
    5. Law Enforcement:Randomly target a group, and some of their partners, depending on group's strength
    6. Repeat
